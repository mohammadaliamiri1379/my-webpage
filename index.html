Spell Checker

I participated in a competition organized by Torob.com for their recommended system project, where I had the opportunity to contribute to the design of a spell checker. The objective of the overall project was to optimize the arrangement of products shown to users, ensuring they click on the first products presented to them. In addition to this goal, we faced the challenge of the "cold start" assumption, meaning we couldn't rely on the user's search history. Throughout the competition, we encountered various obstacles, including query searches with spelling mistakes. Recognizing the significance of accurate matching, I took the initiative to design and implement a spell checker. This spell checker played a crucial role in improving the accuracy and relevance of the search results by identifying and suggesting corrections for misspelled search queries. Moreover, we confronted the challenge of processing a large amount of data within an optimized timeframe. To address this, I focused on developing a suitable topology and refining my code to reduce processing time. Although one particular code required 49 hours to process the data, I continuously worked on enhancing its efficiency. In addition to the spell checker and data processing optimization, we integrated product images into our recommended system. By calculating the proportion of clicks to the number of times a product was shown, especially for popular items, we aimed to predict these proportions for all products using an artificial neural network (ANN) system. This approach allowed us to overcome the bias problem inherent in earlier algorithms, where the results of clicked products were directly influenced by the products shown. While we made significant progress in the development of the recommended system, including the integration of a spell checker, time constraints limited our ability to achieve optimal results in the ANN modeling section of the project.

Position Controller for ZAPI Driver

During my internship at Yektafan Company, I had the opportunity to work on a remarkable project involving the design and implementation of a position controller for the ZAPI driver using Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) technology. The outcome of this project was a product that allowed precise control of an industrial motor's angular position. In this system, the angular position of an industrial motor was obtained through a potentiometer, providing accurate feedback. By utilizing a Proportional-Integral (PI) controller, the motor's position could be precisely regulated to the desired user-defined position. The PI controller parameters were dynamically provided via a BLE system and stored in the Electrically Erasable Programmable Read-Only Memory (EEPROM) for future reference. One of the significant advantages of this system was its versatility. It offered users the flexibility to modify various parameters using their smartphones via Bluetooth connectivity. This capability enhanced the overall user experience and facilitated seamless adjustments to suit specific requirements. The noteworthy achievement of this project was the successful implementation of a comprehensive system capable of controlling the position of any DC industrial motor with the ZAPI driver. The system exhibited exceptional compatibility, supporting a wide range of supply voltages ranging from above 20 volts to below 80 volts. This versatility opened up numerous possibilities for integration into diverse industrial settings. State and Parameter Estimation of DC/DC Converters Based on The Modified Nonlinear Model

The DC/DC switching converters are designed for converting input direct current (DC) voltage to the desired DC voltage [1]. Many applications, such as dc motor drives, computers, and electronic devices, use switch mode power converters [2]. Throughout the years, scientists have suggested different types of modeling for switching systems. However, despite the extensive models like space state averaging [3]–[6], small signal [7], and large signal [8], These methods are only applicable to a smaller class of converters and cannot produce satisfactory results for resonant type converters, so they are only useful around to the operating point [6]. Most articles that worked on estimating states of DC/DC converters use averaged models of the system and neglect the ripple of the capacitor voltage [9]–[13]. So, the deficiency of an accurate model that can explain both CCM and DCM mode is felt. It is apparent that the designed Kalman filter with the aforementioned models cannot estimate accurately and especially the output ripple in the steady state. For instance, [11], [13] assume that sampling frequency is equal to switching frequency, and this assumption causes loose of information between switching frequencies. Considering both CCM and DCM modes is essential for analyzing and causes a wide range of resistance value estimation. In [11], [14] author overlooks the DCM mode of the boost converter, so the controller cannot operate in a wide range of load resistors. Fig. 1. Ideal model of the Buck converter This study uses differential equations to describe both modes and estimate the inductor current, capacitor voltage, and resistance value in a wide load range. The novel advantage of these equations is that they can handle both CCM and DCM mode properly. It also presents an observer extended Kalman filter for monitoring capacitor voltage, inductor current, and resistor value by measuring merely the capacitor voltage in a noisy environment. In this article, by using a new approach for modeling the DC/DC converters and designing an observer based on Extended Kalman Filter, it is shown that the results for the buck, boost, and buck boost converters are satisfying.

Final Thesis

As the culmination of my Bachelor's degree, my final thesis involved the design, implementation, and control of a current source with adjustable amplitude, bandwidth, and frequency. This project was conducted under the supervision of Dr. Farrokhi, who provided valuable guidance and support throughout the process. The primary objective of this project was to develop a current (or voltage) source capable of closely following a user-defined curve. With an operating frequency of 10 Hz, we utilized an Arduino Due microcontroller, programmed using MATLAB Simulink, to achieve the desired control and precision. One of the significant challenges encountered during the project was the presence of process and measurement noise, which could potentially affect the accuracy of the output. To address this issue, we designed a suitable digital filter, specifically a FIR Kaiser filter, which effectively reduced the impact of noise and ensured reliable performance. By successfully integrating the designed current source, implementing control algorithms, and mitigating noise-related issues, this project demonstrated the effectiveness of our approach in achieving precise and adjustable current waveforms. This work not only provided a valuable opportunity for practical application of theoretical knowledge but also deepened my understanding of electronic systems, control theory, and digital signal processing.

Distance-Balancer Mobile Robot

Under the supervision of Dr. Ebadollahi, I worked on a fascinating robotic project called the Distance-Balancer Mobile Robot. The primary goal of this project was to design a robot capable of maintaining a fixed distance from an object. To achieve this, we employed various techniques, including denoising an ultrasonic sensor and implementing a PID (Proportional-Integral-Derivative) controller. By effectively denoising the ultrasonic sensor, we were able to accurately measure the distance between the robot and the object in its surroundings. This data served as the input for the PID controller, which enabled the robot to dynamically adjust its movements to maintain a consistent distance. To fine-tune the PID controller and ensure optimal performance, we utilized model identification techniques specific to the mobile robot. This involved analyzing the robot's behavior, collecting data, and deriving the appropriate PID parameters. The PID tuner functionality of MATLAB played a crucial role in this process, aiding us in finding the ideal set of parameters for the controller.

Liquid Surface Height PID Controller

The project focused on implementing a Liquid Surface Height Controller using a PID (Proportional-Integral-Derivative) approach. The system aimed to regulate and maintain a desired level of liquid in a container. To achieve this, an ultrasonic sensor was utilized to measure the liquid's level, which was then fed back into a closed-loop control system. By integrating the ultrasonic sensor into the setup, we were able to accurately capture real-time data on the liquid's height. This information served as crucial feedback for the closed-loop control system, allowing it to dynamically adjust and control the liquid's level. The implementation of the Liquid Surface Height Controller with PID not only showcased the power of feedback control systems but also highlighted the importance of precise sensing techniques. This project offered a practical solution for maintaining liquid levels in various applications, ensuring optimal performance and efficiency.
